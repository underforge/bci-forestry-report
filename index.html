§ 01 — Ownership 02 / 09
The structure

A chain five links long.

The public pension plans at the top don't directly own any trees. They own units in pooled funds managed by BCI, which holds the equity in the timberland companies, which in turn hold the land. The chain explains both the scale of the exposure and the difficulty of seeing it.

05 — Operator on the ground
Mosaic Forest Management
Plans, harvests, sells. 200 direct employees, ~2,000 contractor workforce, ~C$500M/year paid to contractors. Headquartered Vancouver; operations in Nanaimo, Nanoose Bay, Campbell River.

§ 03 — Timeline 04 / 09
How we got here

Twenty years of consolidation.

The private land base on Vancouver Island was assembled by the 19th-century E&N railway, passed through MacMillan Bloedel, Weyerhaeuser, and Fletcher Challenge, and finally came to rest — largely — in the pension system.

1887
E&N Land Grant
The Crown transfers ~2 million acres of unceded First Nations territory to Robert Dunsmuir and the Esquimalt & Nanaimo Railway. This is the origin of the private-land regime that still governs most of the east coast today.
2005
Island Timberlands is formed
Brookfield and Weyerhaeuser spin out their coastal BC private holdings into Island Timberlands LP. BCI and AIMCo join as limited partners.
2011
BCI + PSP acquire TimberWest for C$1.03B
The publicly-traded TimberWest Income Trust is taken private. From this date forward, there is no public market disclosure of TimberWest's harvest plans, financial performance, or valuation.
2018
Mosaic Forest Management launched
TimberWest and Island Timberlands stay structurally separate but are affiliated under a single operating manager, Mosaic. AIMCo later exits, leaving BCI + PSP as the controlling owners.
2022
BigCoast Forest Climate Initiative
Mosaic announces a 25-year harvest deferral on ~40,000 ha (~7% of its land base) via the sale of carbon credits. Financially, a new revenue stream that converts un-cut trees into cashflow.
2024 — 2026
BC AAC drops to a generational low
Province's Allowable Annual Cut from Crown land falls to 59.6M m³ (from 85.6M in 2007). Provincial budget now projects only 29M m³ over three years. Private land — where Mosaic operates — is untouched by this decline.

§ 05 — Core question 06 / 09
Why so much logging?

Biology grows at 3%.
Pensions want 7%.

The central tension of pension-owned timberland is a simple mathematical gap. If your fiduciary benchmark demands more return than the forest can biologically generate, the gap has to be closed somewhere — and on private land in BC, almost all the levers point toward cutting more, cutting faster, or cutting bigger trees.

The gap, visualized
2 – 4% Natural forest growth rate, east coast VI
6.3% BCI I&RR benchmark, fiscal 2024
7.0% BCI I&RR actual return, fiscal 2024
8.0% TimberWest Income Trust era — investor demand

If the trees only grow 3% a year — the actual biological productivity of the Coastal Douglas-fir ecosystem — and the pension benchmark requires 7%, the extra 4% has to come from somewhere.

The options are narrow: raise productivity (harder logging, mechanization — already near ceiling), cut costs (reduce wages and contractor payments, or squeeze tax), find new revenue streams (carbon credits, real estate conversion), or cut more wood than the forest is currently growing.

"The natural growth rate of timber on the east coast of Vancouver Island is 2–4%, but TimberWest's investors demanded 8%." — Yellow Point Ecological Society
Where the return ends up coming from
Harvest volumes on private land have trended well above biological replacement since the 1990s. Peak extraction (2004–05) coincided with the TimberWest Income Trust's obligation to pay 8% yield to unitholders. The 2011 take-private deal by BCI + PSP did not structurally reduce return expectations — it just made them opaque. The 2018 Mosaic affiliation created cost savings (shared roads, offices, planning), which helps the math. The 2022 BigCoast carbon initiative added a new revenue line: revenue from not logging, rather than from logging. That's the first instrument in 140 years that financially rewards leaving trees standing.
§ 06 — Rules of the game 07 / 09
Private vs. Crown

Two forests,
two sets of rules.

The reason BCI's land can be logged so heavily isn't primarily a BCI decision — it's a regulatory structure. Private Managed Forest Land in BC operates under a stripped-down self-regulation regime. The old-growth deferrals currently reshaping BC forestry policy apply only to Crown land, not to the private E&N lands where Mosaic operates.

Dimension Crown Land (most of BC) Private Managed Forest Land (Mosaic)
Allowable Cut Set by Chief Forester; currently 59.6M m³/yr province-wide (down from 85.6M in 2007). Budget forecasts: 29M m³ over next 3 years. No AAC set by government. Owners choose harvest levels subject to five "management commitments" only.
Old-growth deferrals 2.6M hectares identified by science panel for at-risk old-growth deferrals, pending First Nations consent. Provincial old-growth deferral program does not apply. Includes rare Coastal Douglas-fir ecosystem.
Property tax rate N/A — Crown owned. ~10% of residential rate. No exit fee after 15 years in the PMFL class. Developer-premium sales recoup nothing to the province.
Public scrutiny AAC hearings, Forest Stewardship Plans, Forest Practices Board audits, FOI. Company decides; no public harvest plan required. Forest Practices Board has jurisdiction only on TFLs.
Raw log exports Federal + provincial controls; surplus test before export allowed. Federal-only restrictions. Private land has always exported a larger share. Mosaic: "domestic markets alone cannot sustain harvest."
Indigenous consent DRIPA / UNDRIP obligations; consultation required. Land is unceded but fee-simple; no statutory consent obligation. Mosaic pursues voluntary PAIR certification.

The 2019 BC public review of the Private Managed Forest Lands program drew overwhelming public input about high harvest rates, water, and biodiversity. Nearly six years on, no policy change has been proposed. A FOI release showed confidential meetings between the government, the Private Forest Landowners Association, and Mosaic; the contents were redacted.

§ 07 — The counterweight 08 / 09
The other direction

BigCoast: seven percent
set aside.

In October 2022 Mosaic announced the BigCoast Forest Climate Initiative — a 25-year harvest deferral on ~40,000 hectares (~100,000 acres) of private land. The mechanism is revenue from carbon credit sales. It is the first instrument in the E&N land base that converts standing trees into revenue rather than cut trees.

7.3%
The remaining ~93% of Mosaic land remains available for harvest
40,000 ha deferred ~510,000 ha still in working inventory
What it is

Nature-based carbon credits: Mosaic removes older forest from its harvest baseline, the resulting avoided emissions are packaged and sold to organizations with net-zero commitments. A portion of proceeds flows to the Pacific Salmon Foundation and Indigenous conservation research.

What it isn't

A structural change in harvest rate on the remaining 93%. Not old-growth specific (though old-growth is included). Not a permanent protection — 25 years is the deferral term. Not independent of BCI's return requirements — it generates revenue BCI books into I&RR performance.

The underlying economic logic

BigCoast works inside the pension-return math, not against it. If a hectare of old forest generates more NPV as a 25-year carbon credit than as a clear-cut, the pension benchmark is satisfied without the saw. This is the first structural incentive in the BC coastal timber economy where not logging is the financially dominant choice — and it covers 7% of the land.
